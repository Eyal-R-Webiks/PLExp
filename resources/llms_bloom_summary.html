Understanding Bloom's Taxonomy: The Full Picture

Bloom's Taxonomy is a framework originally developed in education to classify cognitive complexity—essentially, the different levels of thinking required to master a subject. The paper applies this framework to LLM benchmark tasks to answer a critical question: Are we really measuring what we claim to measure about model capabilities?

The taxonomy has two dimensions that work together:

Dimension 1: The Cognitive Process (The "Levels" Everyone Talks About)

This dimension arranges thinking tasks in a hierarchy from simplest to most complex. Each level builds on the previous one, creating a pyramid of cognitive demand. Here's how the paper describes them:

  • Remember (Lower-Order): Can you recall or recognize information as presented? This is pure retrieval—the model needs to find and reproduce what was explicitly stated. Example: "What year was X founded?" where the answer appears verbatim in the text.
  • Understand (Lower-Order): Can you grasp the meaning and explain ideas in your own words? The model must comprehend what was said and communicate understanding without just copying. Example: "Why did the author argue for X?" requires interpreting intent, not just finding a sentence.
  • Apply (Mid-Order): Can you use knowledge in a new but concrete situation? The model must take a rule, concept, or procedure and use it to solve a problem or complete a task. Example: "Using the algorithm described in the paper, what would the output be if input was Y?"
  • Analyze (Mid-Order/Higher-Order boundary): Can you break information into parts and explain relationships? The model must identify components, see how they connect, and possibly spot patterns or contradictions. Example: "Compare the two approaches described. What are their key differences and why do those differences matter?"
  • Evaluate (Higher-Order): Can you make judgments based on criteria? The model must assess the quality, validity, or effectiveness of something using explicit or implicit standards. Example: "Which solution is better and why?" or "Is the evidence sufficient to support the claim?"
  • Create (Higher-Order): Can you put elements together to form something new? The model must synthesize, design, or generate original work (not just recombine existing pieces). Example: "Design a new approach to the problem that addresses the limitations mentioned in the paper."

Key insight from the paper: Current LLM benchmarks cluster heavily in the Remember → Understand → Apply → Analyze range. Almost nothing tests Evaluate or Create. This creates an illusion: models look capable because they're measured where they shine, not where they might struggle.

Dimension 2: The Knowledge Type (Less Well Known But Important)

Beyond the level of thinking, Bloom also classifies the type of knowledge being processed. This is the second dimension. Understanding this matters because a task can be "Remember" level with conceptual knowledge, or "Remember" level with procedural knowledge—and these might require different model capabilities.

  • Factual: Discrete facts, definitions, terminology. "What is the capital of France?" or "Define photosynthesis." These are knowledge items that stand alone. Models tend to do well here if facts appeared in training.
  • Conceptual: Categories, principles, generalizations, relationships. "How do democracy and authoritarianism differ?" or "Explain the relationship between supply and demand." These require understanding how ideas connect and organize. Trickier than pure facts.
  • Procedural: How to do something—steps, techniques, methods, algorithms. "How would you debug a Python function?" or "What are the steps to solve a quadratic equation?" Models must recall a sequence and its logic. This is surprisingly hard for many models when the procedure is complex.
  • Metacognitive: Awareness of your own knowledge and thinking. "What do you know well about this topic and what gaps do you have?" or "How confident are you in this answer and why?" This is almost never tested in benchmarks, and models are generally not trained for it.

Key insight from the paper: Knowledge-dimension labeling was harder to agree on than cognitive-process levels. The authors found that LLMs and humans disagreed more on "What type of knowledge is this?" than on "What level of thinking does this require?" This suggests that the knowledge dimension is either genuinely ambiguous or that models haven't learned to distinguish knowledge types well.

How To Recognize What Level A Task Belongs To: Practical Recognition Guide

The paper emphasizes that mapping tasks to Bloom levels isn't always obvious. Many tasks sit on a boundary or require judgment. Here's what the authors learned about the key decision points:

Remember vs. Understand

  • Remember: The answer appears in the source material, often verbatim or nearly identical. You could find it with a search or pattern match. Example: "I have 3 apples and 2 oranges. How many items do I have?" (from the paper—the model just needs to count what's there).
  • Understand: The answer requires paraphrasing, explaining, or restating in different words. The information is present but not in the exact form needed. Example: "Explain why the author chose this approach" (you must interpret, not just quote).
  • Gray zone: Reading comprehension tasks that ask "What does the passage say about X?" sit here. If the passage almost says it directly, it's Remember-leaning. If you must infer or reword, it's Understand-leaning.

Understand vs. Apply

  • Understand: The model explains what something means or how it works in a general sense. Example: "What is machine learning?" (explain the concept).
  • Apply: The model uses a rule, procedure, or concept to solve a new concrete problem. Example: "Apply the algorithm described in Section 3 to this data." (execute it on a novel instance).
  • Gray zone: Math and coding problems often blur the line. Simple substitution (plug numbers in) is Apply. Complex multi-step problem-solving is also Apply. But if the problem just asks "explain the method," that's Understand.

Apply vs. Analyze

  • Apply: Use a known procedure or rule to solve a straightforward problem. Example: "Solve 2x + 5 = 15 using the method from the textbook."
  • Analyze: Break something into parts, identify components, find patterns, or explain relationships. Example: "Compare two algorithms: Which is more efficient for large datasets and why?" (requires decomposition and comparison, not just execution).
  • Gray zone: Complex procedural tasks (like debugging or designing a system) can be Analyze-level if they require diagnosing what's wrong or explaining trade-offs. The key: does solving it require breaking things down and reasoning about parts?

Analyze vs. Evaluate

  • Analyze: Identify components and relationships: "What are the key differences between approach A and B?"
  • Evaluate: Make a judgment about quality, validity, or preference based on criteria: "Which approach is better and why?" or "Is the evidence sufficient?" (Judge, assess, critique).
  • Gray zone: Many tasks ask students to compare but stop short of judging. "Compare two viewpoints" is Analyze. "Which viewpoint is more credible?" adds Evaluate.

Evaluate vs. Create

  • Evaluate: Assess existing work: "Is this solution good? Does this argument work? Is this code correct?" (Judge based on criteria).
  • Create: Generate something new: "Design your own solution." "Write a different approach." "Propose an improvement." (Synthesize or generate, not just assess existing ideas).
  • Gray zone: The paper notes this is hard to score reliably because "creating" is open-ended. You can rank solutions against each other (Evaluate) but measuring whether a generated solution is truly novel and effective requires subjective judgment.

Examples from the Paper

The paper gives a concrete example: the BIG-Bench task "object_counting" asks "I have a fish, a bear, and a frog. How many animals do I have?" This is pure Remember: count what's given. But within BIG-Bench, "snarks" asks "Which statement is sarcastic?" This is Analyze: recognize sarcasm requires breaking down language and inferring intent. Same benchmark, wildly different cognitive levels—which is exactly why aggregating benchmark scores hides the truth.

What This Imbalance Means

A model can score excellently on all 43 tasks and still never demonstrate ability to:

  • Judge the merits of competing approaches (Evaluate)
  • Generate novel solutions or designs (Create)
  • Make coherent trade-off decisions (Evaluate)

The paper argues that until benchmarks include balanced coverage across all Bloom levels, we cannot claim to be measuring "general LLM capability." We're measuring "capability in a narrow band of cognitive tasks."

Deep Dive: The Knowledge Dimensions and Why They're Tricky

The paper uses a second classification axis that gets less attention but is equally important: the type of knowledge being tested. A task's Bloom level (Remember, Understand, etc.) and its knowledge type work together to define what's really being asked.

Why Knowledge Type Matters

Consider two tasks at the Understand level:

  • Factual/Understand: "What is the definition of photosynthesis?" (You must recall a definition and potentially rephrase it. Models with the definition in training tend to do well.)
  • Procedural/Understand: "Explain the steps for debugging a Python function." (You must recall a procedure and explain it coherently. This is harder—it requires both retrieval and sequencing.)

Both are Understand level, but the procedural task is measurably harder for many models. This is why knowing both dimensions matters—a Bloom level alone doesn't tell the full story.

How to Recognize Each Knowledge Type

Factual Knowledge:

  • Standalone facts, definitions, terminology, specific dates, names, categories.
  • Examples: "When was X invented?" "Define paradigm." "What is the capital of France?"
  • Recognition clue: The task is asking "What is this?" or "When did this happen?" or "Recall this specific item."
  • Models handle this well if the fact appears in training data. If it's obscure, performance drops.

Conceptual Knowledge:

  • Categories, principles, relationships, generalizations, cause-effect chains, systems.
  • Examples: "How do democracy and authoritarianism differ?" "Explain the relationship between price and demand." "What are the main causes of climate change?"
  • Recognition clue: The task asks about relationships, systems, or broader frameworks. It's not just a single fact but a web of connected ideas.
  • Models often struggle more with conceptual tasks that require distinguishing ideas and explaining nuance. They can sound right while missing distinctions.

Procedural Knowledge:

  • Techniques, methods, algorithms, step-by-step processes, "how-to" knowledge.
  • Examples: "How would you debug this Python code?" "What are the steps to solve a quadratic equation?" "Describe the process for conducting a literature review."
  • Recognition clue: The task asks "How do you do this?" or "What are the steps?" It requires sequencing and the logic connecting steps.
  • Interestingly, the paper found procedural knowledge is often harder for models than conceptual. Maintaining logical order and justifying each step is cognitively demanding for LLMs.

Metacognitive Knowledge:

  • Awareness about your own knowledge and thinking: what you know well, what gaps you have, how confident you are, why you used a strategy, what you'd do differently next time.
  • Examples: "What are you most uncertain about in this explanation?" "How confident are you and why?" "Describe your reasoning process."
  • Recognition clue: The task explicitly asks you to reflect on your own thinking, not just answer a question. It's meta—about cognition, not the task itself.
  • This is almost never tested in current benchmarks. The paper found zero benchmark tasks classified as metacognitive. Models also seem unprepared for it—they don't naturally introspect or express epistemic uncertainty well.

The Agreement Problem the Paper Found

The authors had human annotators, LLMs (GPT-4, Claude, Llama), and a fine-tuned classifier label tasks by both cognitive level and knowledge type. Here's what they found:

  • Cognitive level agreement was fairly good: Humans and LLMs agreed on Bloom levels (especially when the task was clearly at one level). Inter-rater agreement was around 0.63 (on a weighted kappa scale).
  • Knowledge type agreement was much weaker: For knowledge dimensions, agreement dropped to about 0.30. Humans and LLMs often disagreed on whether a task was Factual vs. Conceptual, or Procedural vs. Conceptual.
  • Implication: Knowledge dimensions are genuinely ambiguous (a task can reasonably be classified multiple ways), or the models haven't learned to make these distinctions reliably. Either way, it's a signal that this dimension needs more attention and clearer operational definitions.

Knowledge Type Distribution in Benchmarks

  • Factual: 9 tasks (21%)
  • Conceptual: 20 tasks (47%) — the most common
  • Procedural: 14 tasks (33%)
  • Metacognitive: 0 tasks (0%) — absent entirely

Conceptual knowledge dominates, which reflects benchmark designers' focus on understanding and reasoning over pure fact recall or how-to procedures. But the zero metacognitive coverage is notable—tests of self-awareness and confidence calibration are nowhere to be found.

  • Performance is typically stronger on lower Bloom levels.
  • Performance tends to drop as tasks require deeper reasoning (Analyze and above).
  • Aggregated benchmark scores can hide major variation between subtasks.
  • A model can look excellent overall while failing on specific cognitively harder tasks.

What They Found About Model Performance

The authors tested three major models (GPT-4, Llama 3, Claude 3) on their curated task set and looked at performance broken down by Bloom level. The picture was stark:

  • Performance drops significantly as cognitive complexity increases. Models score highest on Remember and Understand tasks, noticeably lower on Apply and Analyze, and (where available) lower still on higher levels.
  • GPT-4 is the strongest but shows the same pattern. It scores ~99% on Remember, ~89% on Understand, ~93% on Apply, but ~76% on Analyze. Even the best model shows a notable cliff at higher levels.
  • Aggregated scores mask this completely. If you report "GPT-4 scores 87% on BIG-Bench Hard," you're hiding the fact that some subtasks are trivial for it and others are genuinely hard. Average gives false confidence.
  • Smaller models struggle even more. Models like Bloomz (3B) and Falcon (7B) show steep drops starting from Apply level, with performance sometimes under 50% at Analyze tasks.
  • The knowledge dimension patterns are interesting: Most models perform better on Conceptual tasks than on Procedural or Factual, contrary to what one might expect. This suggests models have learned general reasoning over rote memorization, which is good, but procedural knowledge (step-by-step, sequential logic) remains a genuine weak point.

What's Really Happening: The Training Explanation

Why do models perform better at lower Bloom levels? The paper theorizes it's baked into training:

  • Next-token prediction (the base training objective) is fundamentally low-level cognition. You're predicting the next word given context—this is closer to Remember/Understand than Analyze/Evaluate/Create.
  • Fine-tuning and reinforcement learning (RLHF) improve things somewhat, but they don't fundamentally retrain the base model to excel at higher-order reasoning. They're refinements, not overhauls.
  • As a result, models inherit a cognitive profile aligned with their training. They're optimized for predicting plausible continuations (Remember), understanding context (Understand), applying patterns they've seen (Apply). But genuinely novel reasoning (Analyze+) requires capabilities that aren't directly rewarded during pretraining.

Why This Is Relevant To LLM Evaluation (Very Directly)

The implications of this research go beyond academic interest. Here's how it directly affects how you should think about model evaluation and selection:

  1. It changes how we interpret "good benchmark scores". If your model scores 90% on MMLU, it doesn't mean it's 90% capable at "understanding." It means it's 90% capable at the subset of cognitive skills MMLU tests, which skews toward Understand/Apply. You're not getting a balanced picture.
  2. It gives a structured way to diagnose capability gaps, not just rank models. Instead of asking "Is Model A better than Model B?" you can ask "Where does Model A excel and where does it struggle cognitively?" This is much more actionable for product decisions and debugging.
  3. It helps you design balanced benchmark suites instead of convenience suites. Most benchmarks grow organically and happen to emphasize what's easy to measure. Mapping to Bloom reveals these biases and lets you intentionally fill gaps.
  4. It makes evaluation reports more credible and actionable. When you report "GPT-4 achieves 76% on Analyze-level tasks," stakeholders get a realistic expectation. When you report only aggregate scores, you invite overconfidence.
  5. It highlights risks in model deployment. If you're deploying an LLM for tasks that require Evaluate or Create, and your evaluation suite has zero such tasks, you've built a blind spot into your system.

Practical Recommendations You Can Use Immediately

If you're responsible for LLM evaluation or selection, here are concrete steps to apply this research:

  1. Report benchmark results by cognitive category, not only by benchmark name. For each major benchmark, break down scores by Bloom level. Yes, it's more work to report, but the granularity is worth it. A single "BIG-Bench Hard score" is almost useless without subtask breakdowns.
  2. Always publish subtask-level scores when benchmarks are heterogeneous. MMLU, BIG-Bench Hard, and AGIEval all have vastly different subtasks with different cognitive demands. Averaging across them creates a false consensus. Break them down.
  3. Intentionally add Evaluate and Create coverage to your evaluation stack before claiming broad capability. If your benchmark suite has zero tasks at these levels, you cannot claim to be measuring "general LLM capability." Add a few tasks that require judgment and generation. (Hint: This is harder to score, but it's worth it.)
  4. Include at least one metacognitive-style test. Ask your model to predict its own performance, or to express confidence with calibration. These are rare in benchmarks but increasingly important for deployed systems that need to know when to defer to humans.
  5. Treat "overall score" as a summary, never as the full story. Always publish a breakdown. A model that scores 85% overall but only 50% on Analyze is very different from a model that scores 85% uniformly across levels.
  6. When comparing models, compare across cognitive levels, not just on total score. Model A might be better at Remember/Understand, while Model B is better at Analyze/Evaluate. Your choice should depend on what your use case needs.

If You Need Prompt Wording (For Your Pipeline)

Ask explicitly for a clear explanation of Bloom’s taxonomy and why that taxonomy matters for LLM evaluation. In practice, this improves answer quality and keeps the output focused on evaluation design rather than generic pedagogy.

Suggested phrasing: “Provide a clear explanation of Bloom’s taxonomy and explain its relevance to evaluating LLM capabilities, benchmark coverage, and interpretation of model performance. Include concrete examples of tasks at each cognitive level and explain how different types of knowledge (Factual, Conceptual, Procedural, Metacognitive) interact with these levels."

Limitations To Keep In Mind

This research is powerful, but it has boundaries. Understanding them helps you apply the framework responsibly:

  • Bloom was designed for education, not LLM evaluation. When educators designed this taxonomy, they were thinking about students and learning objectives. LLM tasks are different—there's no learning process, no metacognition in the human sense. The framework is a useful lens, but it's not a perfect fit.
  • Task difficulty and input length can confound the cognitive-level interpretation. A task that should be "Remember" level can become "Analyze" level if the input is so long that the model struggles to locate the relevant sentence. Context length is a confounding factor. So is task presentation and wording.
  • Knowledge-dimension labeling had much weaker inter-rater agreement. The authors found that humans and LLMs agreed on Bloom levels ~63% of the time, but only ~30% of the time on knowledge dimensions. This suggests the knowledge dimension is either genuinely ambiguous or that the definitions need refinement. Don't over-rely on knowledge-type classifications.
  • Evaluate and Create are hard to score reliably. These levels require subjective judgment. Two annotators might reasonably disagree on whether a generated output is "creative" or whether an evaluation is well-justified. This doesn't invalidate the framework, but it means you'll need clear rubrics if you add these levels to your benchmarks.
  • The paper tested a specific subset of benchmarks. Their analysis covers 43 tasks from commonly cited benchmarks. Your evaluation suite might have different distributions. The overall finding (under-coverage of Evaluate/Create) is likely to generalize, but the specific numbers might not.
